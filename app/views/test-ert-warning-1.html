What are these ratings?

Information used to generate these outputs:

  • Probability of collision: generated based on orbit determination calculations. High represents a probability over 1%; medium over 0.1%; and low below 0.1%.

What do the ratings mean?

  • High: requires urgent action.
  • Medium: monitor closely and notify internal team.

Emergency Response Framework information

  • ERF levels, Emergency Response Framework levels, are defined levels for coordinating a response to an emergency event.
    • Level 1 events are unlikely to have an impact and, therefore, should be monitored over time but require no action.
    • Level 2 events potentially may have a significant impact and so should be actively monitored: the UKSA analyst team should be alerted to analyse the event data more regularly and in more depth; the UKSA SST team should be notified and plan potential next steps; and the ERT should consider passing on this information to other relevant government departments.
    • Level 3 events are rare and require urgent action and high-level coordination between government teams: the UKSA analyst team should be alerted to analyse the event data more regularly and in more depth; the UKSA SST team should be notified and plan potential next steps; and the ERT should consider passing on this information to other relevant government departments.

  • Factors to consider when allocating or editing an ERF level: potential risk to UK assets, other sectors, loss of services, reputational damage and financial loss.

  • Use the ERF levels to inform what actions you should take in response to certain events and how to escalate the response within the UKSA and Government.

Data sources and reliability

  • Data is originally sourced from the US database, Space-track. We have generated a more realistic probability of collision using our own more refined estimating technique. This means that our data outputs are usually more realistic than those generated by Space-track.

  • Data is received from Space-track every 8 hours or so, therefore, you can expect updates once or twice per day.

  • Where possible, we may also task UK radars and sensors to give us rapid tracking data on the objects involved.
